A general-reader map of major fusion facilities in Europe and how they fit into the longer path toward grid electricity.

Key idea: Fusion progress is a pipeline of machines, not a single leap from lab experiments to power plants.

For an exhaustive list, down to university-scale machines, the IAEA’s fusion device database is the best resource available.

Major fusion facilities in Europe

Magnetic confinement

ITER France, Saint-Paul-lès-Durance
International tokamak under construction since 2010, designed to demonstrate \( Q \ge 10 \) burning plasmas.
WEST France, Cadarache
Tokamak focused on long-pulse operation and tungsten-facing components relevant to ITER. Recently set a European long-duration plasma record (22+ minutes).
JET UK, Culham
Europe’s former flagship tokamak. Operations ended in Dec 2023 and the site is moving into decommissioning and repurposing.
MAST Upgrade (MAST-U) UK, Culham
Spherical tokamak testing compact-reactor technologies and the Super-X divertor concept to reduce exhaust heat loads.
ASDEX Upgrade Germany, Garching
Divertor tokamak used to develop physics and operating scenarios relevant to ITER and future reactors.
Wendelstein 7-X Germany, Greifswald
Largest stellarator, built to demonstrate reactor-optimised stellarator concepts aimed at stable confinement and long-pulse operation.
TEXTOR Germany, Jülich, retired
Tokamak that ran for about 30 years and shut down in 2013. Historically important for plasma wall interaction and materials studies.
TCV Switzerland, Lausanne (EPFL)
Variable-configuration tokamak known for flexible plasma shaping to study confinement, stability, and divertor concepts.
TJ-II Spain, Madrid
Flexible stellarator and Spain’s flagship magnetic confinement device.
RFX-mod2 Italy, Padua
Major reversed-field pinch (RFP) experiment emphasising stability and control.
FTU Italy, Frascati, retired
Compact high-field tokamak at ENEA Frascati. The site is being cleared for DTT, with FTU disassembly activities reported in 2024.
DTT Italy, Frascati, under construction
Divertor Tokamak Test facility, focused on divertor technologies at relevant heat loads for next-generation designs.
EXTRAP T2R Sweden, Stockholm (KTH)
RFP facility used heavily for stability studies and active feedback control of resistive wall modes.
ISTTOK Portugal, Lisbon
Compact tokamak used as a flexible research platform including AC-operation studies.
COMPASS-U Czech Republic, Prague, in development
Successor to the COMPASS tokamak programme, being constructed for ITER-relevant research.
GOLEM Czech Republic, Prague
Small tokamak used for education and remote experimentation, operated at the Czech Technical University.

Inertial fusion and high-energy-density facilities

LMJ / PETAL France, near Bordeaux
Laser Mégajoule is a major high-energy-density and inertial-fusion research facility. PETAL adds a high-power petawatt beam coupled to LMJ.
Vulcan 20-20 UK, Harwell Campus (RAL)
Vulcan has been decommissioned to enable the Vulcan 20-20 upgrade for higher power high-energy-density physics and fusion research.
PALS Czech Republic, Prague
Prague Asterix Laser System, a major European laser facility for high-energy-density plasma physics and inertial-fusion science.

The next steps in Europe’s fusion pipeline

ITER’s job in the fusion power pipeline

ITER is not a project to realise commercial fusion, but rather an experiment commissioned to achieve a \( Q > 10 \) plasma gain and help develop the next generation of tokamaks.

ITER’s research plan has been delayed since its conception in the 2010s. In the current timings, the project is estimated to reach full magnetic energy around 2036, with operation beginning around 2039.

What ITER enables:
  • stable, high-power plasmas and reliable control
  • heat exhaust and plasma-facing components under reactor-like conditions
  • validation of models used to design the next machines

DEMO

In Europe’s roadmap, DEMO is framed as ITER’s successor and is seen to be the point where fusion becomes an electricity-producing technology and not just plasma physics.

DEMO’s role is to demonstrate fusion power delivery to the grid, as well as maintainability, the fuel cycle, and tritium breeding. Europe is actively reviewing and maturing DEMO concepts.

STEP: the UK’s prototype power plant

The UK’s STEP (Spherical Tokamak for Energy Production) programme aims for a prototype fusion power plant by 2040, planned for the West Burton site.

Work on spherical tokamaks like MAST-U is directly relevant to compact reactor layouts and divertor technologies that STEP will need to develop at power-plant scale.

Stellarators and alternatives

Tokamaks are widely regarded as the most promising route to DEMO, but Europe is also seriously considering alternatives, especially stellarators. If engineered well enough, stellarators may offer steady-state operation without the same disruption challenges tokamaks need to actively control.

Alongside publicly funded stellarator research (Wendelstein 7-X), Europe also has a private effort spun out from the Max Planck Institute for Plasma Physics: Proxima Fusion. The company is pursuing an optimised, high-field stellarator power-plant concept.

Takeaway

When fusion achieves its goals of large-scale energy to the grid, it is unlikely to be a single machine that appears fully formed.
